## Overview

The technical writing process consists of eight steps designed to create documentation that is accurate, clear, and useful for its intended audience.

---

## Step 1: Start with Audience and Goal

Define the foundational context before writing:

- **Who is the page for?** Developers, customers, internal teams, or a specific subset?
- **What are they trying to do?** What specific task or question brings them to this documentation?
- **What does success look like?** How will they know they've completed the task correctly?

This step sets the right level of detail from the start and helps realign if your approach drifts during writing.

---

## Step 2: Gather Accurate Source Material

Strong technical writing depends on accurate inputs. Don't rely solely on your knowledge.

**Sources to consult:**
- Product specifications and requirements
- Source code and implementation details
- Support tickets and user feedback
- Subject matter experts (SMEs)
- Existing documentation that might be updated rather than duplicated

**Questions to answer:**
- What problem does this documentation solve?
- What task does the reader need to complete?
- What information is essential vs. nice-to-have?
- What examples or screenshots would reduce ambiguity?
- Does related documentation already exist that could be updated?

---

## Step 3: Plan a Search and AI-Friendly Structure

Organize content to be discoverable and scannable.

**Key practices:**
- Use headings that match actual search queries and user intent
- Group related topics together logically
- Keep each page focused on one job, workflow, or concept
- Think about information architecture—how your product or system breaks into topics and subtopics
- Use clear, hierarchical heading structures (H2 for major topics, H3 for subtopics)

**Tools help:** Documentation platforms with code blocks, structured navigation, built-in search, and AI chatbot tools make this easier.

---

## Step 4: Write Clearly and Get to Point Fast

Lead with the answer, then expand with context.

**Writing principles:**
- Use short sentences and direct language
- Maintain consistent terminology throughout
- Avoid jargon or explain it when necessary
- Write the way you speak—natural but precise
- Make it scannable: use lists, bold text, and white space

**Quality check:** Read complex sections aloud. If a sentence feels heavy or too long, rewrite it. Your writing should sound like you explaining the topic in person.

---

## Step 5: Add Examples, Visuals, and Real Workflows

Examples turn abstract explanations into actionable guidance.

**Include:**
- Screenshots showing actual UI and workflows
- Code samples and snippets
- Diagrams and architecture visualizations
- Sample scenarios or real-world use cases
- Sample data or expected outputs

**Principle:** Examples help readers act with confidence by showing what success looks like.

---

## Step 6: Review with Subject Matter Experts

Reviewing is part of the writing process, not a last-minute step.

**Review checklist:**
- **Accuracy** - Are facts correct? Does code work? Is the process accurate?
- **Completeness** - Are all steps included? Are edge cases covered?
- **Clarity** - Is the writing easy to understand? Are terms consistent?
- **Terminology** - Are terms used correctly and consistently?
- **Broken elements** - Check for broken steps, links, code samples, and screenshots

**Participants:**
- Technical teammates validate accuracy
- Strong writers/editors improve clarity and flow
- Original SMEs confirm no details were lost

---

## Step 7: Publish, Measure, and Improve

Documentation doesn't stop at publication.

**Measure:**
- Page analytics and engagement metrics
- User search behavior and search terms
- Support ticket trends and recurring questions
- User feedback and comments

**Improve:**
- Identify underperforming pages that need work
- Look for patterns in where users get stuck
- Update content based on real user behavior, not assumptions
- If your platform has an AI assistant, analyze what users ask it and whether it successfully answers their questions

---

## Step 8: Keep It Current

Technical documentation is never finished. Products change constantly.

**Maintenance practices:**
- Build docs review into every product release process
- Update or flag pages when:
  - Features are added, modified, or deprecated
  - Workflows change
  - UI or APIs are updated
  - New best practices emerge
- Remove outdated content rather than letting it linger
- Document the last review date prominently

**Reality:** This is the best way to keep software documentation accurate over time and maintain user trust.

---

## Common Challenges

- **Time pressure** - Treat documentation like code; don't rush it
- **SME availability** - Start gathering material early
- **Keeping current** - Build maintenance into your process, don't treat it as afterthought
- **Finding balance** - Too little detail leaves users stuck; too much overwhelms them
- **Staying consistent** - Use templates and style guides
